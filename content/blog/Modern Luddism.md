I'm a luddite. You might be, too. If you aren't, you should be. That's what this blog is going to be about. This post is to explain what exactly the word "luddite" means today.

It does not mean that I am against technology. Luddites are not against technology. *Nobody* is against technology. Technology is just the sum total of the material *stuff* we use to live our lives. Every single human being that has ever lived has used, and liked, technology. *Chimpanzees* and *octopuses* have technology {check octopuses}. Technology is not just a smartphone or a spaceplane but also a bicycle, an iron plow, or a wooden stick.

Luddites are also not against *new* technology. Lots of new technology is great. It's hard to find any reason to oppose the fact that every year we seem to have radically cheaper solar panels. If you handed a 10th-century peasant a better plow, they'd thank you for it. Chimpanzees probably go looking for new and better sticks for digging termites out of trees.

The old luddites, the ones who coined the term back in the 19th century British midlands {check}, *were* against steam engines and power looms. Or at least they were against the fact that they were used to impoverish rural communities by taking away the cottage industries they depended on, and giving nothing back. The old luddites were some of the industrial world's first true revolutionaries, and history has been too unkind to them. But I, a modern luddite, am not against steam engines or power looms. That fight is in the past.

 Luddites are against progress. Not against specific ways that our society has "progressed" (i.e., become better according to some metric). Those must be debated on a case-by-case basis. They're against the *concept* of progress. They're against it because it poisons all those debates.

Taken in its most literal terms, the word "progress" simply means getting closer to some goal or endpoint, with no accounting for whether that endpoint is good or bad. Sure, sometimes society makes progress towards some desirable goal, like phasing out fossil fuels or ending racist institutions. But it is equally valid to say that the Russian army has made progress towards conquering the Donbas. Recently, a cancer progressed through my Mom's body until she died. So, if someone uses a thought-terminating cliche like "you can't hold up progress", there's an obvious reply. Progress towards what?

When we talk about society, technology, the economy, and similar Big Important Topics, that question is rarely answered. Progress is just assumed to be the continued forward momentum in society's current direction of travel. This is used as a kind of rhetorical billy-club to silence anyone who challenges that momentum, by fighting against a new data centre or fracking site. It's taken for-granted, and usually left unsaid that these things are an important part of our continued *progress* towards some desirable future. 

The assumption that we are *progressing* towards a desirable future has to be left unsaid, because it is increasingly clear that our future is not very desirable. Progress in our current direction of travel means higher sea levels and more superstorms; minds polluted by social media algorithms and AI slop; bits of plastic from disposable packaging sitting in the Marianas Trench. These facts are embarrassing for the people who want progress, so they cover them up with various band-aid solutions. Electrify cars, but don't build bike lanes. Ban teenagers from social media and pretend that its algorithms are harmless for anyone over the age of 18. Keep walking towards the forest fire, but make sure you put on some kevlar {check} and grab a watering can along the way.

The first luddites spat in the face of this notion of progress. They could see that technology, in the real world, was making their lives worse, and they fought against it. Not against technology or innovation or even power looms, per se. But against the way they were being used to build a new world sucked. They rejected the notion that just because we've been developing in a certain direction, there's a moral imperative to keep going. They wanted an about-face. Or at least a very sharp turn.

Modern luddites share this attitude. We're not against electricity, or microchips, or fertilizers or wireless signals carrying data through the air. We're against the idea that we have to accept *everything that comes along with these things as inevitable and desirable.* We want to progress towards something else.

My notion of modern luddites includes a lot of people that might not, yet, include themselves under this umbrella. It includes [the high-school clubs rejecting smartphones and social media to go read books in the park](). But it also includes the climate activists who can't stomach the idea that continued endless growth and unrestrained consumption is hunky-dory as long as we slap some solar panels on it. It includes the workers, artists, and concerned citizens who don't want our society to be dominated by hallucinating chatbots trained on plagiarized writing. And it includes no shortage of innovators, technophiles, utopians, and idealistic dreamers.

I think there are a lot of strands there that need to be brought together. That's what this blog is going to be about.
